1.2 Firewalls and Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS/IPS)

Firewalls:

Firewalls are network security devices or software programs that monitor and control incoming and outgoing network traffic based on predetermined security rules. They act as barriers between trusted internal networks and untrusted external networks, such as the internet.

Types of Firewalls:
  • Packet-Filtering Firewalls: Inspect packets and permit or block them based on IP addresses, port numbers, and protocols.
  • Stateful Inspection Firewalls: Maintain the state of active connections and make decisions based on traffic context.
  • Proxy Firewalls: Operate at the Application Layer and provide deep inspection of content.
  • Next-Generation Firewalls (NGFWs): Include traditional capabilities plus deep packet inspection and intrusion prevention.

Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS) and Intrusion Prevention Systems (IPS):

IDS and IPS are essential for detecting and responding to network threats.

  • IDS: Monitors traffic for anomalies and alerts administrators about potential attacks.
  • IPS: Can automatically block malicious traffic in real-time.

How IDS/IPS and Firewalls Work Together:

Firewalls control traffic, IDS identifies threats, and IPS blocks malicious activity.

1.3 VPNs and Tunneling Protocols

Virtual Private Networks (VPNs) allow secure communication over public networks by encrypting traffic.

VPN Types:

  • Remote Access VPN: Secure connections for users accessing corporate resources remotely.
  • Site-to-Site VPN: Connects entire networks securely over the internet.

Key VPN Protocols:

  • IPSec: Secures data at the IP layer.
  • SSL/TLS: Commonly used for secure remote access.
  • OpenVPN: Flexible protocol using SSL/TLS encryption.
  • IKEv2: Known for speed and stability in VPN connections.

Tunneling Protocols:

  • GRE: Encapsulates various protocols inside point-to-point connections.
  • L2TP: Works with IPSec for secure connections.
  • PPTP: An older protocol with basic encryption.

1.4 Segmentation and VLANs

Network segmentation divides a network into smaller segments to improve security and performance.

Segmentation Benefits:

  • Reduced Attack Surface: Isolating network segments limits potential compromises.
  • Enhanced Security: Access restrictions hinder lateral movement of attackers.
  • Improved Performance: Optimizes traffic flow and reduces congestion.

VLANs (Virtual Local Area Networks):

VLANs segment a network logically, allowing for better traffic management and security.

Use Cases for Segmentation and VLANs:

  • Guest Networks: Separate guest access from internal resources.
  • Departmental Segmentation: Isolate critical departments from the rest of the network.

2.2 Common Cybersecurity Threats

1. Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs):

Definition: APTs are highly sophisticated, long-term targeted attacks carried out by nation-states or cybercriminal organizations.

Tactics: They use techniques like spear phishing and exploitation of zero-day vulnerabilities.

Goal: Often aimed at data exfiltration or sabotage.

2. Zero-Day Exploits:

Definition: Attacks that exploit unknown vulnerabilities with no official patch available.

Challenges: Extremely dangerous as security tools may not detect them until after an attack.

3. Phishing and Social Engineering:

Phishing: Attackers impersonate legitimate organizations to steal sensitive information.

Spear Phishing: Targeted phishing with personalized messages.

Social Engineering: Manipulating individuals to gain access to confidential information.

4. Ransomware:

Definition: Malware that encrypts files and demands ransom for decryption.

Variants: Some threaten to leak stolen data (double extortion).

5. Distributed Denial of Service (DDoS):

Definition: Floods a network with traffic to overwhelm it.

Tactics: Often uses botnets to launch massive attacks.

3.2 Symmetric vs. Asymmetric Encryption

Symmetric Encryption (Secret-Key Cryptography)

Definition: Uses the same key for both encryption and decryption.

Common Algorithms:

  • AES (Advanced Encryption Standard)
  • DES (Data Encryption Standard)
  • 3DES (Triple DES)

Advantages: Speed and efficiency for large data.

Challenges: Key distribution and scalability.

Asymmetric Encryption (Public-Key Cryptography)

Definition: Uses a pair of keys (public and private).

Common Algorithms:

  • RSA (Rivest–Shamir–Adleman)
  • ECC (Elliptic Curve Cryptography)
  • Diffie-Hellman (DH)

Advantages: Secure key distribution and scalability.

Challenges: Slower than symmetric encryption and limited use for large data.

Use Cases:

Symmetric: Bulk data encryption.

Asymmetric: Key exchange, digital signatures, secure email.

Hybrid Cryptography:

Combines symmetric and asymmetric encryption for optimal security and performance.

3.4 Hashing Algorithms

4.2 Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Definition:

MFA is a security measure that requires users to provide two or more verification factors to gain access to a resource.

Factors of Authentication:

  • Something You Know: Typically a password or PIN.
  • Something You Have: A physical device, such as a smartphone or hardware token.
  • Something You Are: Biometric data, like fingerprints or facial recognition.

How MFA Works:

  1. User enters their username and password.
  2. The system requests a second factor (e.g., OTP).
  3. If successful, access is granted.

Advantages of MFA:

  • Increased security
  • Reduced risk of phishing
  • Compliance with regulations

Challenges of MFA:

  • User resistance
  • Implementation complexity
  • Potential for lockout

4.3 Access Control Models

Role-Based Access Control (RBAC)

Definition: RBAC assigns permissions based on user roles.

How It Works: Roles are defined based on job functions, and users inherit permissions based on their roles.

Advantages: Simplicity, scalability, and compliance.

Challenges: Role explosion and inflexibility in dynamic environments.

Attribute-Based Access Control (ABAC)

Definition: ABAC uses attributes to make access decisions.

How It Works: Access is based on user, resource, and environmental attributes.

Advantages: Granularity and dynamic access control.

Challenges: Complexity and performance impact.

5.2 Types of Malware

1. Viruses

A virus attaches itself to legitimate programs or files, replicating and spreading when executed. It often spreads through email attachments or infected downloads.

Example: ILOVEYOU virus

2. Worms

Worms are standalone malware that replicate themselves to spread across networks without needing to attach to another program.

Example: Conficker worm

3. Trojans

A Trojan disguises itself as a legitimate application to trick users into installing it.

Example: Zeus Trojan

4. Ransomware

Ransomware encrypts files on a victim's system and demands a ransom for the decryption key.

Example: WannaCry

5. Spyware

Spyware monitors user activity and collects sensitive information.

Example: Keyloggers

6. Adware

Adware delivers unwanted advertisements to a user's device.

Example: Browser toolbars

7. Rootkits

Rootkits allow unauthorized users to gain control of a computer without being detected.

Example: Rootkits that execute files and access logs

5.3 Reverse Engineering Malware

Static Analysis

This involves examining the malware code without executing it, looking at file structure, headers, and embedded strings.

Tools: PEiD, BinText, Strings

Dynamic Analysis

This involves executing malware in a controlled environment to observe its behavior in real-time.

Tools: Process Monitor, Wireshark, Regshot

Behavioral Analysis

Analysts observe changes made to the system by malware to understand how it interacts with resources.

Code Analysis

This involves examining the malware code using disassemblers or decompilers.

Tools: IDA Pro, Ghidra

Considerations:

  • Legal and ethical compliance
  • Required skills in programming and system architecture

5.4 Sandboxing

7.2 Incident Handling Process

Definition: Incident handling is a structured approach to manage and resolve security incidents.

  4. Eradication: Remove root causes, malware, and vulnerabilities from the environment.
  5. Recovery: Restore affected systems and monitor for further compromise.
  6. Lessons Learned: Post-incident review to improve processes and update the IRP.

8.1 Shared Responsibility Model

The shared responsibility model is fundamental to cloud security. It defines the distribution of security duties between the cloud service provider (CSP) and the customer to ensure comprehensive protection of cloud-based resources.

Cloud Service Provider (CSP) Responsibilities:

  • Infrastructure Security: The CSP secures the physical infrastructure of the cloud.
  • Platform Security: Providers secure the hypervisors, storage, compute, and network services.
  • Compliance and Certifications: Providers maintain certifications like ISO 27001 and SOC 2.

Customer Responsibilities:

  • Data Security and Access Control: Customers are responsible for encrypting data and managing access.
  • Application Security: Customers must secure applications running in the cloud.
  • Operating Systems and Configurations: Customers manage security for IaaS environments.
  • Network Traffic Management: Customers configure firewalls and access controls.

8.3 Container Security

Containers and their orchestration platforms, like Kubernetes, have become a popular method of deploying applications in the cloud. Containers encapsulate applications and their dependencies, providing portability across environments. However, they introduce new security challenges that must be addressed to ensure a secure cloud deployment.

Key Areas of Container Security:

Container Image Security:

  • Image Scanning: Regularly scan container images for vulnerabilities.
  • Use Official and Trusted Images: Always use vetted images.
  • Avoid Privileged Containers: Do not run containers with elevated privileges.

Kubernetes Security:

  • Network Policies: Control communication between pods.
  • Pod Security Policies (PSP): Enforce security standards at the pod level.
  • RBAC in Kubernetes: Manage access to Kubernetes resources.
  • Secrets Management: Use tools to store sensitive information securely.

Runtime Security:

  • Behavioral Monitoring: Monitor container runtime behavior for anomalies.
  • Image Signing: Ensure container images are digitally signed.
  • Host Security: Secure the underlying OS.

Container Orchestration Security:

  • Secure the Control Plane: Use network segmentation and strong access controls.
  • Limit Node Communication: Restrict communication between nodes.
  • Audit Logs: Enable logging for Kubernetes clusters.

9.1 Regulations and Standards

Various frameworks guide organizations in implementing effective cybersecurity controls. These regulations and standards are often industry-specific, ensuring that security practices align with best practices and legal requirements.

1. National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST)

The NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF) is a widely adopted framework developed by the U.S. government to help organizations manage and reduce cybersecurity risks. It focuses on five core functions:

  1. Identify: Understand business environments, assets, risks, and roles.
  2. Protect: Implement safeguards to ensure service delivery.
  3. Detect: Develop capabilities to detect cybersecurity events.
  4. Respond: Implement response plans for security incidents.
  5. Recover: Ensure timely restoration of systems post-incident.

Use Case: Commonly adopted by U.S. federal agencies and private-sector organizations.

2. ISO/IEC 27001

ISO 27001 is an international standard for Information Security Management Systems (ISMS). Key areas include:

  • Access Control
  • Asset Management
  • Incident Response
  • Encryption and Data Protection

Use Case: Used globally by companies to secure information assets.

3. General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)

GDPR governs the processing of personal data in the EU, with key aspects like:

  • Lawful Data Processing
  • Data Subject Rights
  • Breach Notification

Use Case: Mandatory for companies processing personal data of EU residents.

4. Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA)

HIPAA protects health information confidentiality, requiring:

  • Privacy Rule
  • Security Rule

Use Case: Essential for organizations in the healthcare industry.

5. Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard (PCI DSS)

PCI DSS protects payment card information during transactions. Key objectives include:

  • Build and Maintain a Secure Network
  • Protect Cardholder Data
  • Maintain a Vulnerability Management Program

Use Case: Required for businesses handling payment card transactions.
